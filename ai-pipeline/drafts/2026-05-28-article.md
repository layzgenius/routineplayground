## INTRODUCTION: THE GAME YOU'RE ALREADY PLAYING

You have been scored in every conversation you have had today.

Not by a conscious evaluator with a clipboard — but by the pattern-recognition circuitry that every human brain runs automatically, beneath the level of language, before a single word has been exchanged. Status is assessed in the first 300 milliseconds of an interaction. It is updated continuously as the interaction unfolds. And it determines, more than any other single factor, whether you will be listened to, trusted, advanced, or overlooked.

The frustrating reality is that most people experience the *consequences* of status dynamics without ever understanding the mechanism. They sense that something invisible is being tracked. They notice that certain people command rooms effortlessly while others struggle despite technical competence. They feel the shift when an interaction goes poorly — the drop in energy, the way attention moves away from them — but they cannot name what caused it.

This document names it.

What follows is a complete architectural map of how status is assessed, maintained, compounded, and destroyed in modern professional and social environments. This is not motivational content. It is a technical manual — built on behavioral science, evolutionary psychology, and pattern recognition from thousands of hours of documented human interaction — designed to give you conscious access to a system that has always been running in the background.

The goal is not to make you manipulative. It is to make you literate.

**The scoring system is already running. This document gives you the rulebook.**

## SECTION ONE
# The Two-Hierarchy Model: Dominance vs. Prestige

### Why Most People Are Playing the Wrong Game

Every human group organizes itself along two fundamentally different status hierarchies. Most people operate as if there is only one. This mistake costs them everything.

---

### HIERARCHY TYPE 1: DOMINANCE

Dominance hierarchies are ancient. They predate complex language. They operate on a single currency: **the credible threat of cost imposition**.

A dominance-based high-status individual maintains their position by controlling resources, punishing non-compliance, and generating compliance through fear or social pressure. Think: the person who interrupts constantly, the manager who uses information asymmetry as a weapon, the peer who signals status by making others feel small.

**How dominance status is maintained:**
- Controlling who speaks and when
- Withholding approval or recognition as a power tool
- Exploiting social friction to establish hierarchy
- Punishing challenges publicly and disproportionately
- Monopolizing attention without reciprocation

**The structural problem with dominance:**

Dominance generates *compliance* — not respect. The moment coercive leverage is reduced (job changes, social shifts, information equalizes), dominance status collapses instantly. It is expensive to maintain, creates adversaries, and produces brittle social architectures. The people beneath a dominance-based leader comply because they must — and leave the moment they can.

---

### HIERARCHY TYPE 2: PRESTIGE

Prestige hierarchies are uniquely human. They require sophisticated social cognition and extended time horizons.

A prestige-based high-status individual maintains their position by **demonstrating competence, generating value, and providing reliable signals of trustworthiness**. Others defer to them voluntarily — not because they fear consequences but because they genuinely benefit from the association.

**How prestige status is maintained:**
- Consistent delivery on stated positions
- Genuine investment in others' outcomes
- Demonstrated competence without insecurity-driven display
- Comfort with complexity and uncertainty
- Selective use of social capital

**The structural advantage of prestige:**

Prestige compounds. Every demonstration of competence, every kept commitment, every instance of genuine generosity or intellectual contribution adds to a growing account that others track subconsciously. Prestige-based status survives context changes, survives the absence of formal power, and creates allies rather than adversaries.

---

### THE CRITICAL INSIGHT

> **Most people who are trying to "be more confident" or "project more authority" are unconsciously deploying dominance signals — generating short-term compliance while eroding long-term respect.**

The table below shows the behavioral fingerprints of each hierarchy:

| Behavior | Dominance Signal | Prestige Signal |
|---|---|---|
| Being challenged | Escalates, punishes | Engages with curiosity |
| In disagreement | Talks over, dismisses | Asks clarifying questions |
| When uncertain | Performs certainty | Acknowledges, investigates |
| With attention | Monopolizes | Distributes strategically |
| Under pressure | Reactive, defensive | Measured, deliberate |
| With success | Claims credit | Attributes to team |
| With failure | Deflects blame | Owns cleanly |

**Assessment question:** In your last five high-stakes interactions, which column were you operating from?

## SECTION TWO
# The Status Signal Stack: What Is Actually Being Read

### The Architecture of First Impressions and Sustained Assessment

Status is not assessed on a single dimension. It is calculated across a layered stack of signals, processed simultaneously by the evaluator's nervous system. Understanding this stack is the difference between guessing and knowing.

---

### LAYER 1: SOMATIC SIGNALS (Pre-Verbal, Highest Weight)

These signals are processed before language, carry the highest baseline weight, and are the hardest to fake.

**Key somatic signals:**
- **Movement economy**: High-status individuals move with deliberate efficiency. Low-status individuals display nervous excess movement — fidgeting, over-gesturing, self-touching (neck, face, arms).
- **Spatial occupation**: Status correlates with the comfortable occupation of available space. Physical contraction signals submission in every mammalian species, including humans.
- **Vocal pace**: Dominant voices are slower. The brain registers deliberate pacing as comfort with authority. Rushed speech reads as anxiety — anxiety reads as low status.
- **Eye contact calibration**: Not sustained staring (dominance signal, often read as aggression) — but relaxed, held contact during key statements. Breaking eye contact downward signals submission; breaking sideways signals confidence.
- **Reaction latency**: How quickly you respond to social pressure. Immediate capitulation = low status. Deliberate pause before response = high status.

---

### LAYER 2: ATTENTIONAL SIGNALS (What You Direct Attention Toward)

The brain tracks what you track. Your attention is a currency, and how you spend it broadcasts your status calculation of every person in the room.

**High-status attentional patterns:**
- Directing attention to ideas and outcomes rather than performing engagement
- Choosing *when* to give attention rather than reflexively responding to all bids
- Not laughing at everything, not agreeing reflexively, not performing constant approachability
- Withholding full attention as a conversational resource (not as manipulation — as genuine selectivity)

**Low-status attentional patterns:**
- Laughing before the punchline to signal approval
- Over-nodding to signal engagement
- Immediate response to every conversational bid regardless of value
- Seeking to catch the eye of high-status individuals (broadcasts upward looking)

---

### LAYER 3: LINGUISTIC SIGNALS (Word Choice, Structure, Framing)

**High-status linguistic patterns:**
- **Declarative over interrogative**: "We should do X" vs. "I was thinking maybe we could do X?"
- **No pre-emptive apology**: "This might be wrong, but..." signals insecurity before the idea is even evaluated
- **No seeking permission for opinions**: "I think X" not "Would it be crazy to think X?"
- **Economy of words**: Low-status speakers over-explain, pad, and justify. High-status speakers state and allow silence to work.
- **First-person ownership**: "I decided" vs. "The decision was made"

**Common high-cost linguistic errors:**
1. Upspeak (rising inflection on declarative statements — converts statements into questions seeking approval)
2. Filler over-use (signals real-time insecurity: "um, like, you know...")
3. Excessive hedging ("kind of", "sort of", "maybe", "possibly" — erodes perceived conviction)
4. Permission-seeking preambles ("I just wanted to say..." / "This is probably dumb but...")

---

### LAYER 4: SOCIAL FRICTION RESPONSE (Highest Diagnostic Value)

How a person responds to social friction — challenge, disagreement, interruption, dismissal — is the single most diagnostic status signal available to an observer.

**The status is revealed in the response, not the provocation.**

| Social Friction Event | Low-Status Response | High-Status Response |
|---|---|---|
| Interrupted mid-sentence | Stops, defers | Continues without acknowledging |
| Opinion challenged | Backs down rapidly, agrees | "Walk me through your thinking" |
| Work criticized | Defensive explanation | "Where specifically?" |
| Dismissive treatment | Tries harder to impress | Neutral, unreactive |
| Joke at your expense | Laugh too hard or visible discomfort | Brief smile, redirect |
| Ignored in group | Louder, more insistent | Patient, strategic |

**The principle underlying all of these:**

High-status individuals have *internalized* their value. External calibration events — praise, challenge, dismissal — do not produce outsized emotional responses because the internal signal is stable. Low-status individuals are constantly reading external signals to determine their own worth, which makes them reactive, and reactivity is one of the clearest low-status signals the brain can read.

---

### LAYER 5: CONSISTENCY SIGNALS (The Reliability Score)

Status is not a single event. It is a running average. The brain tracks consistency between what someone says and what they do — between stated values and actual behavior — with remarkable precision.

**Consistency destroys more status than almost any single event:**
- Changing stated opinions immediately when challenged (regardless of whether the challenge is valid)
- Saying one thing in public and behaving differently in private
- Reliability failures: being late, not following through, saying "I'll look into that" and not looking into it
- Mood-dependent performance: high on good days, low on bad — signals that the internal signal is unstable

## SECTION THREE
# Behavioral Scoring Patterns: Real-Time Calculations Others Run on You

### The Six Scoring Categories and How Points Are Lost

The brain's status assessment isn't a single number — it's a composite score across six behavioral domains, updated continuously.

---

### DOMAIN 1: ENTRY PRESENCE

How you enter a physical space sets the opening bid for all subsequent status calculations. This is a priming effect — the brain anchors to the first signal and adjusts from there.

**Status-positive entry pattern:**
- Pause briefly at the threshold (micro-pause signals comfort, not urgency to be seen)
- Move deliberately, at 80% of your natural pace
- Orient to the room rather than immediately searching for approval points
- Acknowledge on your own schedule, not reflexively

**Status-negative entry patterns:**
- Rushing (signals you are over-scheduled or undervalued)
- Immediate approval-seeking (scanning for who noticed you arrived)
- Apology-entry ("Sorry I'm late, the traffic was...") — opens with a deficit
- Loud overcompensation (performing energy to signal confidence you don't feel)

---

### DOMAIN 2: CONVERSATIONAL ARCHITECTURE

Every conversation has a structure — who asks, who answers, who sets topics, who follows. Status is embedded in this architecture.

**High-status conversational patterns:**
- Asking high-quality questions (signals confidence in curiosity over need to perform)
- Comfortable with pauses — not filling every silence
- Not over-explaining after making a point (state, then stop)
- Challenging premises rather than only adding information
- Using silence as punctuation

---

### DOMAIN 3: DISAGREEMENT HANDLING

Most people either avoid disagreement entirely (low status: signals inability to hold a position) or escalate aggressively (dominance: generates compliance, erodes respect). The prestige-based model is neither.

**The high-status disagreement protocol:**
1. Receive the disagreement without visible reactivity (the pause is part of the signal)
2. Confirm understanding before responding: "So your position is X — is that right?"
3. State your position declaratively without over-justifying
4. If the challenge has merit, update publicly and own the update: "You're right — I hadn't considered that angle"
5. If the challenge lacks merit, hold your position calmly: "I've looked at this from that angle and I don't think it changes my read"

The critical principle: **willingness to update is high status; reflexive updating is low status.** The difference is whether the update is driven by logic or by social pressure.

---

### DOMAIN 4: ACKNOWLEDGMENT PATTERNS

How you distribute acknowledgment, compliments, and credit tells others exactly how you calculate your own status relative to them.

**High-status acknowledgment:**
- Specific ("That analysis was sharp — particularly the cost comparison")
- Not reflexive (you don't acknowledge everything; therefore acknowledgment means something)
- Delivered without qualification ("That was excellent work" not "That was actually pretty good")
- Attributing success to others when it genuinely belongs there

**Low-status acknowledgment:**
- Excessive, reflexive praise (signals you need the other person to like you)
- Complimenting effort over results (patronizing when used as a hedge)
- Adding qualifications that diminish ("Not bad for a first attempt")

---

### DOMAIN 5: INFORMATION HANDLING

How you handle information — sharing it, withholding it, using it — is one of the most nuanced status domains.

**High-status information behavior:**
- Sharing valuable information proactively (prestige building — signals security)
- Not name-dropping or credential-stacking (signals insecurity)
- Comfortable saying "I don't know" (security in ignorance signals high internal status)
- Not demonstrating knowledge unnecessarily (competence speaks through outcomes, not display)

**Low-status information behavior:**
- Over-credentialing ("In my 12 years of experience...")
- Name-dropping to borrow status from associates
- Withholding information to maintain power (dominance play — perceived and resented)
- Refusing to admit ignorance (insecurity that everyone can read)

---

### DOMAIN 6: REACTION CONTROL

The speed and amplitude of your emotional reactions is one of the most biologically readable status signals.

**High-status reaction profile:**
- Surprised by events but not destabilized
- Responsive to genuine injustice but not reactive to social friction
- Proportionate emotional expression (not flat — that reads as either suppression or contempt)
- Full emotions expressed through words and reasoning rather than behavioral explosion

**Low-status reaction profile:**
- Disproportionate emotional response to small provocations
- Visible anxiety when status is ambiguous
- Performing calm while exhibiting somatic stress signals
- Emotional explosions followed by shame (oscillation signals internal instability)

## SECTION FOUR
# The 12 High-Status Behavioral Defaults

### Patterns That Compound Respect Over Time

These are not techniques to deploy situationally. They are behavioral defaults — the automatic patterns of genuinely high-status individuals. The goal is to internalize them until they require no conscious effort.

---

**DEFAULT 1: COMPLETE BEFORE RESPONDING**

High-status individuals listen until the other person is fully finished — including the pause after the last word. This signals that you are not planning your response while the other person is speaking, and that you have enough internal security that you don't need to be the first to fill silence.

---

**DEFAULT 2: HOLD POSITIONS UNDER PRESSURE UNLESS LOGIC CHANGES**

The distinction: update when the reasoning changes, not when the social pressure increases. If someone simply repeats a challenge louder or more insistently, that is not new information — and updating signals that social pressure is a valid tool to use on you.

---

**DEFAULT 3: MOVE AT YOUR OWN PACE**

In social environments, pace signals internal state. Rushing signals overwhelm or anxiety. Deliberate movement signals comfort with one's position. This applies to walking, speaking, responding to messages, and making decisions.

---

**DEFAULT 4: DISTRIBUTE ATTENTION SELECTIVELY**

Attention is your most finite resource. Distributing it reflexively to every bid signals that you have no selection criteria — which signals that your attention is not particularly valuable. Genuine high-status individuals give their attention fully when they give it, and withhold it when they are not genuinely engaged.

---

**DEFAULT 5: OWN IGNORANCE CLEANLY**

"I don't know" is one of the highest-status phrases available. It signals security — you do not need to appear omniscient to feel secure in your position. Performed certainty is instantly readable as insecurity, and it makes every other statement less credible.

---

**DEFAULT 6: SPEAK LAST IN GROUP SETTINGS**

The person who speaks last in a group discussion reads the room, integrates perspectives, and delivers a synthesized view — a pattern that signals both intelligence and status comfort. The person who speaks first is performing. The person who speaks last is leading.

---

**DEFAULT 7: GIVE CREDIT BEFORE CLAIMING IT**

The counterintuitive reality: publicly attributing success to others does not diminish your status — it amplifies it. It signals security (you don't need the credit), leadership (you see the contributions of others), and trustworthiness (you will do the same for anyone working with you).

---

**DEFAULT 8: CHALLENGE PREMISES, NOT JUST CONCLUSIONS**

Low-status individuals engage with the conclusions of arguments. High-status individuals examine the premises. Asking "what are we assuming here?" or "is that the right question?" signals a qualitative different level of engagement that immediately elevates perceived status.

---

**DEFAULT 9: DISENGAGE FROM STATUS COMPETITIONS**

When someone is visibly competing for status — trying to establish dominance, making one-up moves, broadcasting credentials — the high-status response is not to compete. It is to refuse to play. Not with contempt, not with withdrawal — but with genuine disengagement. You signal that you are not tracking the scoreboard they are keeping.

---

**DEFAULT 10: REPAIR CLEANLY WITHOUT PERFORMANCE**

When you are wrong, when you have failed, when you have caused harm — repair directly and briefly. "I was wrong about that" or "I handled that poorly" delivered without performance, without excessive explanation, without seeking reassurance. Clean repair signals internal security. Extended apology-performance signals that you need the other person's forgiveness to feel okay — which inverts the status dynamic.

---

**DEFAULT 11: CONTROL YOUR FIRST RESPONSE**

The most important moment in any high-stakes interaction is the 1-2 seconds after something challenging happens. Your first response sets every subsequent social calculation. Practicing deliberate latency — a breath, a pause, a reorientation — before the first response to any significant social input is one of the highest-leverage behavioral investments available.

---

**DEFAULT 12: SET THE FRAME**

Whoever defines the terms, the metrics, and the context of an interaction leads it. High-status individuals establish frames: "The question I'm trying to answer is..." / "The way I'm thinking about this is..." Frame-setting is not aggression — it is leadership. Others will generally adopt the frame offered unless it serves them poorly.

## SECTION FIVE
# Status Threats and Recovery: How Respect Is Lost and Rebuilt

### Understanding Status Damage and Restoration

Status is not permanently damaged by most single events. It is damaged by *patterns* — and it can be rebuilt through deliberate behavioral change. Understanding the mechanisms of both damage and repair is essential to long-term status management.

---

### HOW STATUS IS MOST COMMONLY LOST

**Loss Pattern 1: Reactive Capitulation**
Repeatedly changing positions when challenged — regardless of the quality of the challenge — signals that social pressure works on you. Once this is established in a social group's model of you, you become someone whose stated positions are treated as opening bids rather than genuine views.

*Recovery:* Begin holding positions under pressure. The first few instances will feel uncomfortable. The discomfort is the recalibration happening.

---

**Loss Pattern 2: Approval Accumulation**
Attempting to build status by accumulating approval from high-status individuals — laughing at everything they say, agreeing with their positions, seeking proximity — is immediately readable as low-status positioning. High-status individuals are practiced at detecting this, and it makes you less interesting rather than more.

*Recovery:* Replace approval-seeking behaviors with contribution behaviors. Ask a sharp question. Disagree with a minor point genuinely. Bring information they don't have.

---

**Loss Pattern 3: Over-Explanation**
Explaining your decisions, your reasoning, your qualifications more than the situation requires signals that you expect your position to be challenged — which signals that you believe it should be. High-status individuals make statements. They offer elaboration only when asked.

*Recovery:* Practice the "state and stop" discipline. Make your point. Stop. Let the silence work. Resist the urge to fill it with justification.

---

**Loss Pattern 4: Emotional Volatility**
Disproportionate emotional reactions — to criticism, to being overlooked, to small provocations — signal that your internal calibration is unstable and externally dependent. These events are visible, memorable, and update everyone's model of you significantly.

*Recovery:* The pause protocol. Every time you feel a reactive impulse, a deliberate pause before any response. Over time, the gap between stimulus and response becomes your most reliable status asset.

---

**Loss Pattern 5: Inconsistency Between Stated Values and Behavior**
This is the highest-cost status damage available. Once observers register that your behavior does not match your stated values, every subsequent statement is evaluated through that filter.

*Recovery:* Identify the specific inconsistencies and address them behaviorally — not with statements about your values, but through observable behavior change. Status reputation repairs slowly but reliably.

---

### THE STATUS RECOVERY PROTOCOL

When significant status damage has occurred, the following sequence produces the fastest credible recovery:

1. **Acknowledge the actual damage** — to yourself first, with specificity. "I handled that poorly" is the beginning.
2. **Identify the specific behavior** — not "I was too emotional" but "I capitulated when challenged and over-explained my reasoning."
3. **Make one clean repair** — if the damage was interpersonal, one direct acknowledgment (not extensive apology) closes the loop.
4. **Behavioral change, not announcement** — do not announce that you are changing. Simply change. Let the behavioral signal carry the message.
5. **Consistency window** — status repairs on a 30-60 day behavioral consistency horizon. This cannot be accelerated, but it can be maintained.

## SECTION SIX
# Environmental Status Calibration: Context Changes the Rules

### The Same Behavior Reads Differently in Different Environments

Status is not computed against an absolute standard. It is computed against the contextual baseline of the environment. Understanding how context changes the scoring rules allows you to calibrate rather than apply a single behavioral template.

---

### HIGH-STAKES PROFESSIONAL ENVIRONMENTS

**Baseline calibration:**
- Precision and economy of language carry maximum weight
- Reputation travels in advance — your track record is part of every status calculation
- Credibility is stake-based: those who have delivered are given more signal weight
- Being right more often than wrong is the compounding asset

**Calibration priorities:**
- Substance over style (style matters but is secondary to demonstrated competence)
- Meeting commitments at 100% reliability (consistency is the core prestige signal)
- Asking high-quality questions over making statements when uncertain

---

### SOCIAL AND PEER ENVIRONMENTS

**Baseline calibration:**
- Warmth and generosity carry more weight than in professional contexts
- Social comfort and ease are primary signals
- The ability to make others feel seen is a high-prestige behavior in this context
- Humor — used as connection rather than dominance — is a status amplifier

**Calibration priorities:**
- Genuine curiosity about others (not performed interest)
- Contribution to group energy without monopolizing it
- Being the person who creates good experiences for others

---

### COMPETITIVE/ADVERSARIAL ENVIRONMENTS

**Baseline calibration:**
- Emotional regulation is the primary differentiator
- Being moved less than others by the stakes is a status signal
- Decision speed under pressure signals competence
- Showing comfort in the environment where others show stress

**Calibration priorities:**
- Pre-calibrate emotional state before entering the environment
- Practice the pause protocol specifically for high-pressure moments
- Compete on outcomes, not on dominance signals

---

### HIERARCHICALLY ASYMMETRIC ENVIRONMENTS (Operating UP and DOWN the org chart)

**Operating with high-status others (upward interactions):**
- Do not perform humility — it signals that you believe their status should diminish you
- Do not perform equal status — it signals insecurity about your actual position
- Engage as a peer in substance while acknowledging structural difference in process
- Bring genuine value — the highest status move available in asymmetric interactions

**Operating with lower-status others (downward interactions):**
- Generosity of attention is one of the highest prestige signals available
- Genuine interest in their perspective builds the kind of loyalty that compounds
- Not using structural power to extract compliance (dominance) vs. building genuine investment (prestige)

## SECTION EIGHT
# The 30-Day Prestige Protocol

### A Structured Behavioral Calibration Plan

This protocol installs the four highest-leverage status behaviors through deliberate practice. Each week adds a new behavior while maintaining prior weeks.

---

### WEEK 1: THE PAUSE PROTOCOL

**Practice:** Insert a 1-3 second deliberate pause before responding to any significant social input — questions, challenges, requests, criticism.

**Daily implementation:**
- Before responding to the first question in any meeting: pause, breathe, respond
- When challenged: stop, let 2 seconds pass, then engage
- When asked for a decision you haven't made: "Let me think about that" rather than immediately responding

**What this installs:** Reaction latency as a default. The gap between stimulus and response that signals internal security.

---

### WEEK 2: THE STATE-AND-STOP DISCIPLINE

**Practice:** After making any point, statement, or opinion — stop. Do not fill the subsequent silence with elaboration, justification, or qualification unless directly asked.

**Daily implementation:**
- After making a recommendation in any context: state it once, then stop
- After being challenged: hold the pause, then respond — but respond once, not three times
- After a disagreement: state your updated or unchanged position, then stop

**What this installs:** Economy of language. Statements that don't trail off into self-undermining elaboration.

---

### WEEK 3: ATTENTIONAL DISCIPLINE

**Practice:** For one full day each week, track every instance of reflexive approval behavior — automatic laughing, over-nodding, immediate response to every conversational bid — and consciously interrupt the pattern.

**Daily implementation:**
- In conversations: give attention when genuinely engaged, not as performance
- In group settings: contribute when you have something to add, not to signal participation
- With compliments: be specific and genuine, not reflexive

**What this installs:** Attentional selectivity. The signal that your engagement and approval mean something.

---

### WEEK 4: THE FRAME-SETTING PRACTICE

**Practice:** In three interactions per week, set the frame explicitly before engaging: "The question I'm working on is..." / "The way I'm approaching this..." / "What I'm trying to understand here..."

**Daily implementation:**
- In meetings: offer a framing statement before the group settles into someone else's
- In disagreements: reframe the terms of the discussion ("I think the real question is...")
- In negotiations: establish your evaluation criteria before the other party establishes theirs

**What this installs:** Habitual frame-setting. The automatic leadership behavior that defines interactions rather than responding to them.

## SUMMARY: THE COMPLETE STATUS MAP

### The Five Principles That Govern Everything

---

**PRINCIPLE 1: Status is behavioral, not positional.**
Title, money, and credentials create the *appearance* of status but not the *reality*. Behavioral signals are assessed continuously and override credential signals in most real-time interactions.

---

**PRINCIPLE 2: Prestige compounds. Dominance decays.**
Every prestige-based behavior — genuine contribution, consistency, clean repair, generous acknowledgment — adds to a growing account. Every dominance-based behavior — coercion, credit-claiming, volatility — creates temporary compliance at the cost of long-term respect erosion.

---

**PRINCIPLE 3: Reactivity is the universal low-status signal.**
The single most readable status signal is how strongly external events move you. The more your internal state is shaped by external inputs — praise, challenge, dismissal — the lower the status signal you broadcast. Internal stability is the foundation of all other status signals.

---

**PRINCIPLE 4: Consistency is the delivery mechanism.**
Intelligence, competence, and good intentions produce no status benefit without behavioral consistency. The brain tracks pattern, not performance. One exceptional interaction is a data point. Twelve consistent interactions are a reputation.

---

**PRINCIPLE 5: The frame holder leads.**
